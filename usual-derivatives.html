Context

Derivatives are the core of the analysis field and allow us to study how functions vary. Differentiation as a whole is a helpful and versatile tool, widely used in mathematics, physics, and economics.

The basic intuition behind differentiation is simple: we look at the result of a function at two input values, and find out by how much it has varied. To have a more precise result, we take two closer points. This process does not stop at any fixed choice of points, but considers what happens as the two points get arbitrarily close to each other. By following this reasoning, we end up choosing points which are separated by \(h\), a variable which is approaching 0. This is the intuition behind the mathematical definition of a derivative at a given point.

In practice, we rarely use this limit definition to compute derivatives of common functions. Instead, we rely on differentiation rules, summarized in the following table.

Basic Differentiation Rules

The derivative of a function f(x) is another function, often called f'(x). We should also note that we differentiate with regards to a variable. In the following table, we differentiate f(x) with regards to x.

f(x) f'(x)
k (Constant) \(0\)
x \(1\)
kx \(k\)
x^n \(n \cdot x^{n-1}\)
e^x \(e^x\)
ln(x) \(1/x\)
\(k^x\) \(k^x \cdot \ln(k)\)

We should also note that the derivative of a sum is equal to the sum of the derivatives of each individual component. This also works for subtractions. This property is especially important, as it allows us to treat each component of a sum individually when computing derivatives. This can be written as:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\big[f(x) + g(x)\big] = \frac{d}{dx}f(x) + \frac{d}{dx}g(x) \]

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\big[f(x) - g(x)\big] = \frac{d}{dx}f(x) - \frac{d}{dx}g(x) \]
